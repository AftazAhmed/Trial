Have you ever noticed sparks coming out of loose plugs, switchboards, and other electrical appliances? During winter, you may have experienced a spark when rubbing or removing woollen or polyester clothes with a jerk. Lightning is also a type of spark that we see in the sky during the rainy season. Despite air being a poor conductor of electricity, lightning still occurs. To understand how this happens, we need to first grasp the concept of electric charges.
âš¡ ELECTRIC CHARGE
We have learnt in an earlier chapter about the negative and positive charges inside an atom. The positively charged particles in an atom are called protons while negatively charged particles are called electrons. The atom is said to be electrically neutral when it contains an equal number of electrons and protons which is usually the case. If, however, the charges are not balanced, the object will have an electric charge (positive charge if protons are more in number than electrons and negative charge if electrons are greater in number than protons).

ğŸ”‹ Charging an Object
Objects around us are electrically neutral as atoms have an equal number of positive and negative charges. Electrically neutral objects can be charged by the following methods.
Charging by Friction:
This is done by rubbing one material with another. Rubbing a glass rod with silk and an ebonite rod with wool are the most common examples. When a glass rod is rubbed with silk, the negative charges get transferred to the silk and the glass rod is left with positive charges. Similarly, when the ebonite rod is rubbed with fur, the negative charges go from the wool to the ebonite rod. Therefore, the ebonite rod becomes negatively charged and the wool is left with a positive charge.
